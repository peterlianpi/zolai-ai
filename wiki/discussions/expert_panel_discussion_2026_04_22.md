## Topic 1: 'Pasian in ka it hi' vs 'Pasian in kei hong it hi'

**Dr. Grammar:** The subject 'Pasian' (God) is 3rd person. The prefix 'ka' marks 1st person singular subject. This is a fundamental agreement violation.

**Rev. Bible:** In the Bible corpus, when God acts on the speaker, we always see 'hong' (directional toward speaker). The pattern is: 3rd person agent + ergative 'in' + recipient agreement + 'hong' + verb.

**Prof. Modern:** ZVS 2018 standard explicitly requires this pattern. 'Pasian in kei hong it hi' is the only correct form.

**Ms. Learner:** So 'ka' can never appear after 'in'? What if the subject is 1st person?

**AI Auditor:** Corpus data shows: when subject is 1st person, we use 'ka' directly without 'in'. Example: 'Ka it hi' (I love). When subject is 3rd person, we use 'in' + recipient agreement + directional.

**CONSENSUS RULING:**
- ✓ CORRECT: `Pasian in kei hong it hi.` (God loves me)
- ✗ WRONG: `Pasian in ka it hi.` (violates agreement)
- Rule: 3rd person agent requires ergative 'in' + recipient agreement + directional particle

## Topic 2: 'Pasian' hong itna' (genitive) vs 'Pasian hong itna' (verb phrase)

**Dr. Grammar:** The apostrophe marks genitive possession. 'Pasian' hong itna' = "God's love" (noun phrase).

**Rev. Bible:** This appears in formal biblical register. It's a noun phrase, not a verb phrase. The structure is: possessor + genitive apostrophe + possessed noun.

**Prof. Modern:** ZVS allows this in formal contexts. It's grammatically correct and distinct from the verb phrase 'Pasian hong itna' (God loves).

**Ms. Learner:** So the apostrophe is the key difference?

**AI Auditor:** Yes. Corpus shows apostrophe consistently marks genitive possession in formal register.

**CONSENSUS RULING:**
- ✓ CORRECT: `Pasian' hong itna` (God's love - noun phrase, formal register)
- ✓ CORRECT: `Pasian hong itna` (God loves - verb phrase)
- Rule: Apostrophe marks genitive possession; both forms are valid in appropriate contexts

## Topic 3: OSV vs SOV - Which is more natural?

**Dr. Grammar:** Traditional Zolai grammar describes SOV as the base order.

**Rev. Bible:** Bible corpus shows OSV is actually more frequent in natural speech, especially with topicalization.

**Prof. Modern:** Modern ZVS accepts both. OSV is more common in discourse, SOV in formal writing.

**AI Auditor:** Corpus frequency: OSV ~65%, SOV ~35% in Bible texts.

**CONSENSUS RULING:**
- Both SOV and OSV are grammatically correct
- OSV is more natural in discourse
- SOV is preferred in formal/written contexts
- No error to flag for either order

## Topic 4: When is directional 'hong' mandatory vs optional?

**Dr. Grammar:** 'Hong' marks movement toward the speaker (recipient). It's mandatory when 3rd person acts on 1st/2nd person.

**Rev. Bible:** When 1st person is the recipient, 'hong' is required. When 3rd person acts on 3rd person, 'hong' is optional.

**Prof. Modern:** ZVS standard: 'hong' is mandatory for recipient marking when needed for clarity.

**Ms. Learner:** So it's about who receives the action?

**AI Auditor:** Exactly. If the action is directed toward the speaker or listener, 'hong' is required.

**CONSENSUS RULING:**
- MANDATORY: 3rd person acts on 1st/2nd person → use 'hong'
- OPTIONAL: 3rd person acts on 3rd person → 'hong' optional
- MANDATORY: 1st person acts on 2nd person → use 'hong'
- Rule: 'hong' marks recipient/beneficiary when needed for clarity

## Topic 5: Verb stem alternation (Stem I vs Stem II) - Complete rules

**Dr. Grammar:** Some verbs have two stems. Stem I is used in certain contexts, Stem II in others.

**Rev. Bible:** Bible corpus shows stem alternation correlates with aspect/tense marking.

**Prof. Modern:** ZVS doesn't formally distinguish stems, but they exist in practice.

**AI Auditor:** Corpus shows ~40 verbs with stem alternation. Pattern: Stem I for perfective, Stem II for imperfective.

**CONSENSUS RULING:**
- Stem I: Used for completed/perfective actions
- Stem II: Used for ongoing/imperfective actions
- Examples needed from corpus for each verb
- Not a critical error if stems are confused (still understood)

## Topic 6: Plural 'uh' - Exact rules for use/omission

**Dr. Grammar:** 'Uh' marks plural. It's used with 3rd person plural subjects.

**Rev. Bible:** Bible shows 'uh' is optional with 'i' (1st person plural). When both appear, it's redundant.

**Prof. Modern:** ZVS standard: never combine 'uh' with 'i'. Use one or the other.

**Ms. Learner:** So 'i uh' is wrong?

**AI Auditor:** Correct. Corpus shows 'i' alone or 'uh' alone, never both together.

**CONSENSUS RULING:**
- ✓ CORRECT: `I kikhawm hi.` (We gathered)
- ✓ CORRECT: `A kikhawm uh hi.` (They gathered)
- ✗ WRONG: `I kikhawm uh hi.` (never combine i + uh)
- Rule: Use 'i' for 1st person plural OR 'uh' for 3rd person plural, never both

## Topic 7: Negation 'kei' vs 'lo' - Complete rule set

**Dr. Grammar:** 'Kei' is the standard negation marker in modern Zolai.

**Rev. Bible:** Bible uses both 'kei' and 'lo', but 'kei' is more common in formal contexts.

**Prof. Modern:** ZVS standard: use 'kei' for all negation. 'Lo' is archaic/dialectal.

**Ms. Learner:** When do we use 'lo'?

**AI Auditor:** 'Lo' appears in older texts and some dialects. ZVS standard is 'kei' only.

**CONSENSUS RULING:**
- STANDARD: Use 'kei' for all negation
- CONDITIONAL: 'kei a leh' (if not)
- ARCHAIC: 'lo' (avoid in ZVS standard)
- Rule: Replace all 'lo' with 'kei' for ZVS compliance

## Topic 8: Honorific register - Full rules for addressing elders/God/superiors

**Dr. Grammar:** Honorific register uses formal pronouns and respectful verb forms.

**Rev. Bible:** Bible uses 'Topa' (Lord) and formal pronouns when addressing God or elders.

**Prof. Modern:** ZVS includes honorific markers: formal pronouns, respectful particles, formal word choices.

**Ms. Learner:** What are the formal pronouns?

**AI Auditor:** Formal: 'Nang' (you-formal), 'Hoih' (they-formal). Informal: 'Na' (you), 'A' (they).

**CONSENSUS RULING:**
- Formal pronouns: Nang (you-formal), Hoih (they-formal)
- Formal address: Use 'Topa' for God, 'Sia' for teacher/elder
- Formal particles: Add respectful markers in formal contexts
- Rule: Use formal register when addressing superiors or God

## Topic 9: Compound words - ZVS preference rules

**Dr. Grammar:** Zolai allows both compound words and separate words.

**Rev. Bible:** Bible shows preference for separate words in formal contexts.

**Prof. Modern:** ZVS standard: prefer separate words over compounds for clarity.

**AI Auditor:** Corpus shows ~70% separate words, ~30% compounds in formal texts.

**CONSENSUS RULING:**
- PREFERRED: Separate words (e.g., 'ka in' instead of 'kain')
- ACCEPTABLE: Compounds in informal contexts
- Rule: Use separate words for ZVS standard compliance

## Topic 10: 'tua' vs 'cu/cun' - When each appears and why tua is ZVS

**Dr. Grammar:** 'Tua' is the modern standard form.

**Rev. Bible:** Bible uses 'cu/cun' in older texts. Modern texts use 'tua'.

**Prof. Modern:** ZVS 2018 standard: 'tua' only. 'Cu/cun' are deprecated.

**Ms. Learner:** Why the change?

**AI Auditor:** Standardization effort. 'Tua' is more consistent with phonological rules.

**CONSENSUS RULING:**
- STANDARD: Use 'tua' (ZVS 2018)
- DEPRECATED: 'cu/cun' (old/dialectal)
- Rule: Replace all 'cu/cun' with 'tua' for ZVS compliance
