# Prophetic Books Linguistic Survey (Isaiah - Daniel)

This document details the linguistic patterns, rhetorical structures, and pedagogical utility of the Major Prophets (Batch 4) for the Zolai AI curriculum.

## Overview
The Prophetic books represent the peak of Zolai formal rhetoric, switching between emotive judgment oracles and grand visionary prose. They are primarily targeted at **B2 (Intermediate-High) to C1 (Advanced)** learners.

---

## 1. Isaiah (Grand Rhetoric & Visionary Royalty)
Isaiah uses high-register Zolai to describe the majesty of God and the gravity of judgment.

### Key Linguistic Patterns
*   **Grand Commissioning:** Use of the first-person response to divine calls.
    *   *Example:* "Keimah hih sungah ka om hi, kei hong sawl in." (Isaiah 6:8) - *Here am I, send me.*
*   **Contrasting States:** Before and After/Judgment and Restoration.
    *   *Example:* "Na mawhna-te a san' hangin, vu bangin hong paak ding hi." (Isaiah 1:18) - *Though your sins be red, they shall be white as snow.*
*   **Royal Titles:** Use of complex honorifics like "Vanglian Pasian," "Nopna Kumpi" (Prince of Peace).

### Pedagogical Utility
- **Level:** C1 (Advanced Rhetoric)
- **Focus:** Abstract concepts, grand metaphors, and formal commitment language.

---

## 2. Jeremiah (Emotive Prose & Personal Reluctance)
Jeremiah provides a model for expressive, emotive Zolai, focusing on the "Lung" (heart/mind) and personal suffering.

### Key Linguistic Patterns
*   **Expression of Inadequacy:** Model for polite refusal/reluctance.
    *   *Example:* "Thu gen ka siam kei hi; naupang khat ka hi lel hi." (Jeremiah 1:6) - *I cannot speak; for I am a child.*
*   **Infidelity Metaphors:** Intensive use of `paktat` (promiscuity) to describe spiritual straying.
*   **Direct Vision Inquiries:** "Jeremiah aw, bang na mu hiam?" (Jeremiah 1:11) - *Jeremiah, what do you see?*

### Pedagogical Utility
- **Level:** B2 (Intermediate-High)
- **Focus:** Expressing emotions, self-reflection, and moral reprimand.

---

## 3. Ezekiel (Visionary Symbolism & Divine Authority)
Ezekiel introduces highly detailed, technical descriptions of visions and symbolic actions.

### Key Linguistic Patterns
*   **Detailed Descriptive Chains:** Long sentences describing anatomy and appearance.
    *   *Example:* "Maitang li leh kha li nei ciat-te hi." (Ezekiel 1:6) - *Each had four faces and four wings.*
*   **Symbolic Objects:** High utility for "Object-based Narration" (Scroll, Tile, Iron Pan).
*   **Divine "Watchman" Formula:** `Galvilpa` (Watchman) provides a model for responsibility and duty.

### Pedagogical Utility
- **Level:** B2-C1 (Technical Description)
- **Focus:** Spatial descriptions, complex multi-clause sentences, and visionary vocabulary.

---

## 4. Daniel (Court Narrative & Apocalyptic Sequencing)
Daniel is unique for its shift between clear "Court Narrative" and complex "Apocalyptic Timelines."

### Key Linguistic Patterns
*   **Court Etiquette:** Formal address to authorities.
    *   *Example:* "Kumpipa aw, na khan’ sau ta hen!" (Daniel 2:4) - *O King, live forever!*
*   **Resolution and Purpose:** Patterns for setting goals or boundaries.
    *   *Example:* "Daniel in ... a pumpi a thanghuai sak loh nadingin a lungsim a khentat hi." (Daniel 1:8) - *Daniel purposed in his heart not to defile himself.*
*   **Comparative Superiority:** `...sangin a zah sawmin a siamzawk...` (Ten times better than...).

### Pedagogical Utility
- **Level:** B1 (Narrative) to B2 (Moral Resolution)
- **Focus:** Polite address, sequencing events, and expressing conviction.
