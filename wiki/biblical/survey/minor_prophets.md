# Minor Prophets Linguistic Survey (Hosea - Malachi)

This document details the linguistic patterns, sharp rhetoric, and pedagogical utility of the Twelve Minor Prophets (Batch 5) for the Zolai AI curriculum.

## Overview
The Minor Prophets (Hosea through Malachi) are characterized by **concise, sharp prophetic warnings** and **vivid local metaphors** (agriculture, weather, locusts). They are ideal for **B1 (Intermediate) to B2 (Intermediate-High)** learners, focusing on moral imperatives and logical consequences.

---

## 1. Hosea (Marriage & Commitment)
Hosea uses the metaphor of marriage and fidelity to describe the relationship between God and Israel.

### Key Linguistic Patterns
*   **The Marriage Formula:** Using `Zi` (Wife) and `Pasal` (Husband) as a framework for loyalty.
    *   *Example:* "Amah in ka zi hi buang a, kei in ama pasal hi buang hilo ka hi hiam?" (Hosea 2:2) - *She is my wife... am I not her husband?*
*   **Relational Contrast:** `Cihtak` (Faithfulness) vs `Paktat` (Infidelity/Promiscuity).
*   **Repentance Call:** `Hong pai un, Topa kiangah ciahkik ni.` (Hosea 6:1) - *Come, let us return to the Lord.*

### Pedagogical Utility
- **Level:** B1-B2 (Relational vocabulary)
- **Focus:** Expressing commitment, betrayal, and reconciliation.

---

## 2. Amos (Social Justice & Natural Warnings)
Amos uses "Street Zolai" — direct, earthy, and uncompromising — to challenge social inequality.

### Key Linguistic Patterns
*   **Judgment Sequence:** `...a khialh kikkik nilohna hangun...` (For three transgressions... and for four...). This is a model for counting repeated failures.
*   **Socio-Ethic Logic:** Describing the exploitation of the poor.
    *   *Example:* "...khedap bu khat man a loh zawh loh man bekin mi cimawhte sila-in zuak-te hi." (Amos 2:6) - *They sell the poor for a pair of shoes.*
*   **Rhetorical Questions:** Using logic to prove a point.
    *   *Example:* "Mi nih thukimna om masa lopi-in, lampi-ah tonkhawm ahi hiam?" (Amos 3:3) - *Can two walk together, except they be agreed?*

### Pedagogical Utility
- **Level:** B2 (Social Ethics)
- **Focus:** Logical reasoning, counting/sequencing, and sharp moral imperatives.

---

## 3. Malachi (The Dialogic Debate)
Malachi is almost entirely structured as a Q&A between God and the people, making it a masterpiece of dialogic Zolai.

### Key Linguistic Patterns
*   **The "Wherein?" Pattern:** People question a statement, and God provides proof.
    *   *Example:* "Topa in, 'Note kong it hi,' ci hi. Ahi zongin note in, 'Kote hong bangci it na hi hiam?' na ci-te hi." (Malachi 1:2) - *Lord: "I loved you." People: "How have you loved us?"*
*   **Challenge of Faith:** `Kei hong ze-et dih un.` (Malachi 3:10) - *Test me now.*
*   **Legacy and Lineage:** Patterns for inter-generational relationships (Pate leh Tapate).

### Pedagogical Utility
- **Level:** B2 (Argumentative/Dialogic)
- **Focus:** Asking questions, providing evidence, and formal debate.

---

## 4. Other Minor Prophets (Locusts, Dust, and Storms)
The remaining books (Joel, Obadiah, Jonah, Micah, Nahum, Habakkuk, Zephaniah, Haggai, Zechariah) contribute specific thematic vocabulary.

### Key Themes
- **Joel:** The Day of the Lord as a natural disaster (`Kauphe` - Locusts).
- **Jonah:** Direct narrative and reluctance (`...ka tai mang hi.` - *...and I fled.*).
- **Habakkuk:** Questioning divine silence (`Bang tan vei... na ngaih kei ding ahi hiam?` - *How long... will you not hear?*).
- **Zechariah:** High visionary/symbolic vocabulary, similar to Ezekiel but more concise.
